Seattle

We’ve just moved to Seattle. Although I’ve already done moves from British Columbia (my home town, Surrey) to Tulsa, Oklahoma, and then from Tulsa to Minneapolis, Minnesota, this is definitely the hugest move I’ve ever done. When I moved from BC to Tulsa, I was living in a furnished single room that I rented from a family; so I left all my boxes behind and moved with a single suitcase and a duffle bag. Or something like that. When I moved up to Minneapolis, I had been in Tulsa for a few years, but I still moved with a minimum of “stuff”: everything fit into the Nissan Sentra that I was driving at the time.

This move, Amy and I have been married for 11+ years, have a 2 year old, and all kinds of stuff. What started as a plan to use a single 16’ PODS container morphed into me renting a 26’ UHaul, packing it to the gills, and then driving that 1700 miles towing a Mazda CX-9 on a trailer. Let’s say I’ve decided to rule out “long distance truck driver” from my list of prospective mid-life crisis career changes.

That said, everything has gone pretty well. Made the drive in about 2 1/2 days. The folks at UHaul on MLK way were simply awesome, allowing me to park the (full) UHaul in their lot until I was able to actually move into the new house, giving me some additional small discounts, and just generally being an absolute pleasure to work with. They’re getting a Christmas card this year. We had a friend of a friend of a friend find some guys who were willing and able to help unload the UHaul on a weekday, even knowing ahead of time that we had a piano (we have a piano).

Everything is still a little chaotic, but it’s coming together. Looking forward to going to http://seattlerb.org tomorrow, if I can.

A Year From Now, You’ll Look Back at Your Current Self and Realize You’re an Idiot

A recurring realization throughout my childhood was looking back at the things I had done or said or been interested in the year or years prior, and cringing to myself; being alternatively amused, ashamed, or dismayed by the sort of things I had thought or done. At nine, I thought 8-year old me was a bit of a doofus. At 10, I realized that 9-year old me was still a bit goofy. At 13, I figured out that pre-teen me was not very cool. At 15, I thought 13-year old me was a bit of a sycophant (disclosure: I don’t think I actually knew the word “sycophant” when I was 15). In my 20s, I thought teenage-me was totally clueless. By my late 20s, I saw that that period of cluelessness should be expanded to include my early 20s.

And so on.

Through today. Even looking back a few years ago, I’m bemused by things I may have thought or said, or dismayed by thoughts of how I could have handled given situations in a better way.

What’s hardest to realize is that, a year or two from now, I’ll look back at me today and see all (a lot of) the ways in which I’m currently being an idiot.

Why is this?

Eric Barker talks a bit about it here, which is what reminded me about this. But this is something I think about periodically, which hopefully prevents me from believing that I’ve become too smart: in a year, or a little more, I’ll realize all too well just how smart I’m not right now.

Alternate FizzBuzz Solution

If you aren’t familiar with what ‘FizzBuzz’ refers to, see http://c2.com/cgi/wiki?FizzBuzzTest.

Q: So here is a description of the ‘FizzBuzz’ problem. Do you think you could give us a quick solution for this?

A: Sure, that looks pretty simple. I can solve it any way I like?

Q: Of course.

A: Great. Well, I’ll start by writing a unit test.

Q: … Well, that probably isn’t necessary…

A: Maybe not, but I want to be able to instantly verify the solution. Let’s see. Well, to test the output, I’ll need a reference string. [Begins writing] Something to compare the output to show that it’s correct… So, like, “1 2 Fizz 4 Buzz Fizz 7 8 Fizz Buzz 11 Fizz 13 14 FizzBuzz…” [Continues writing]

Q: Well… Okay…

A: Does this look right? [Displays finished string of numbers 1..100 with Fizz & Buzz appropriately inserted] As in, if our output matches this, we’ll have a successful program?

Q: Sure, that looks correct, but…

A: Great. Now let’s write our function. Here’s our method; we’d run the test here and watch it fail. Now, just to make it green, I’ll just have the method output our reference string.

Q: …

A: Say, this actually fulfills the requirements right now. I guess I’m done.

Q: But you actually haven’t written anything to programmatically produce the FizzBuzz string!

A: True, but since we already have a string which we’ve agreed is the correct result, if we just output that string, we’ll satisfy the requirements for the problem. So, I’d say we’re done.

Q: But what if we wanted to see the results from 1 to 10,000?

A: Well, gee. That wasn’t in the requirements.
